My head isn't aching nor is my neck, but they're not not aching either. The
table I'm sat on slants slightly down towards the pedestrianised road and
there is a big menu pinned to the wall next to my head. "Can I have a grosses
bier" _nods_ "and ein Pizza Vegeterienne" _puzzled_ "Veg-gee-tarian" _puzzled_
"Vegetariana" smiles and walks away "Grazzi, I mean Thanks". A marching jazz
band just walked down the street and people are "whoooo" and shouting like a
mobile carnival that has passed now further up the street. Innsbruck is
extremely busy. I'm sitting in a pizzeria outside and my beer just arrived.
I'm on my second beer of the evening after checking into the Montagu Bed and
Beers hostel. I didn't realise it was "and beers" until this afternoon and
that made me worry it was going to be a piss-up venue. But the dormitory is
probably the best designed dormitory I've seen and it's rather empty. The
barman let me have a beer for €2.20 because happy hour ended 5 minutes before
I ordered.

Today has been a hard day. I knew the distance was going to be 80 miles and I
also knew that the course was 60% or more "unpaved" which could mean one of
many things but it doesn't mean fast asphalt and it would involve 16
individual climbs of which 1 is a so-called "category 1" climb of 700m.

This morning I was in Oberstdorf. My calves hurt. I needed a rest day
for my rest day because the 10k run that I did in my barefoot shoes. The run
destroyed my calves and they were still tender in the morning and the 80 mile
gravel ride hasn't mended them, in-fact they are worse. Which _does not_ bode
well for my intention to run the Innsbruck Parkrun tomorrow at 9am.

> this pizza arrived. it's not a great pizza Vegetariana and costs €16.50. I
> don't care though. If I look up past the crowded street and above the 5
> story building at the end of it I see a jagged snow-capped mountain.

I was right to take the rest day and let the cloudy grey day pass, today was
a mostly perfect day for cycling. There was both sun and cloud and alternating
exposure or shade of the shadow of the mountains.

The first climb of the day would be the Alterjochstrasse. Used, apparently, in
Roman times to move troops to Bregenz. The computer registered a 20% gradient
and I slowly eeked my way up the trail past numerous Alpine cows.

![Camera/IMG_20260508_094852_802.jpg](202605061803-cowclimb.jpg)
_Cow Climb_

Climbing the 20% gradient was especailly tricky because my chain wouldn't stay
in the correct gear and would slip this way and that way. I stopped to attempt
to diagnose the problem, I couldn't see anything physically wrong with it but
liberally applying chain lubricant to it seemed to fix it. The rain the day
before yesterday probably didn't help.

The biggest climb of the day would be the
[Zugspitze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zugspitze). I was mildly alarmed in
the morning when I saw that the ascent _and_ descent would largely on trails -
alarming because I knew that the Strava courses were not necessarily _viable_
courses and also it occured to me that there may still be a significant amount
of snow (there wasn't).

Hikers passed me. Cyclists passed me. I normally smile but I realised that
_trying_ to smile when my face is contorted with effort is both difficult and
ineffective as I look like
[hide-the-pain-harold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hide_the_Pain_Harold). So
I changed my strategy to just raise my hand but to otherwise keep going "keep
powering up" one hiker said to me.

I achieved an altitude of around 1700m.

The descent was a joy for the first miles. It was cold but tolerably so and
the gravel was predictable enough and the gradient wasn't too steep and the
road was open enough to let go of the breaks quite liberally.

At around this time the forestry track led to a number of parked cars and then
the road was all but blocked by Austrians at 1100m having some sort of BBQ. I
skidded to make some noise and announce my presence and said "hello", they
moved out of the way but then one Austrian with wobbly eyes and a wobbly beer
said something to me "Haar raa vor gee see ba ch?" I looked non-plussed,
looked at his beer and his unfocused gaze, and tried to move on through. He
took mock offense and as I was riding away grabbed my saddle bag causing me to
fall over onto a dog. I swore and got up and tried to move on but he slided
into my view again "Ausweiss bitte!" and again he grabbed the bag before a
woman stopped him and I cursed again and rode out of reach from the drunk
Austrians to check that everything was still on my bike. Austrians like to
drink in the mountains.

![Camera/IMG_20260508_161016_806.jpg](202605061803-nowtherebewater.jpg)
_Now there be flow_

My laptop bag fell off and then I somehow, and in a controlled way, fell off
my bike to land on my unsteady feet. It was odd because I didn't hit anything
in a particularly violent way, but the bar that holds my phone and computer
had made its way down to the button the unlocks the bag over the course of the
day.

![Camera/IMG_20260508_172406_488.jpg](202605061803-bagfall.jpg)
_The bag that fell off_

The final descent was on  a trail and it was _really_ steep. Remembering my
experience with exploding tires and V-Brakes I was slightly worried at the
extent to which I was using my breaks while descending - but letting go of
them seemed like suicide on the uneven gravelly tracks with sharp 90 degree
turns - or at least an invitation to lose my laptop over the side of the
mountain. But after "falling off" I felt the temperature of the "disk" of the
disk-brake and it was hot, but not so hot that I couldn't keep my finger on
it.

When I the trail finally dropped onto a main rood I let out a "whoooop" of
relief. I was glad that descent was over.

![Camera/IMG_20260508_173913_886.jpg](202605061803-mineralwater.jpg)

At this point howeer there was a strong headwind and it was 8 miles to
Innsbruck - and it felt like an eternity.
